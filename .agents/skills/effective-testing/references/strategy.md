# How to Do a Test Strategy

A test strategy is the set of ideas that guide your testing. It's not a static document; it's an ongoing exploration.

## Strategy Entry Points
When you start testing, you can enter the strategy from any of these 12 points (Chapter 4):
1. **What are you here for?** (Mission).
2. **What do you need to learn?** (Information).
3. **What is happening right now?** (Context).
4. **How is your testing constrained?** (Constraints).
5. **What testing has been done already?** (History).
6. **How is the product being built?** (Process).
7. **What is the product?** (Structure/Function).
8. **How important is your testing?** (Value).
9. **How will people most likely use the product?** (Usage).
10. **What testing is easy to do?** (Efficiency).
11. **What do people expect you to do?** (Expectations).
12. **What's fun to do?** (Engagement).

## Developing the Model
1. **Model the Product**: Use the HTSM guidewords.
2. **Analyze Risk**: Identify what could go wrong. Use the **Four-Part Risk Story**:
   - **Vulnerability**: A weakness in the product.
   - **Threat**: An event that could exploit that weakness.
   - **Impact**: The consequence of that event.
   - **Story**: A narrative that connects them.
3. **Prioritize**: Focus on the highest risks first.

## Practices Worth Practicing
- **Product Coverage Outline (PCO)**: A hierarchical list of things to test.
- **Lenses of Testing**: Look at the product from different perspectives (e.g., Security lens, Usability lens).
- **Be a Problem-Solver, Not a Task-Doer**: Your goal is to solve the problem of "How do we know if this is good?", not just check boxes.

## Final Thought
Good test strategy is more than just following behind development. It's about being an independent investigator of the truth.
